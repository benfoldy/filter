Nonprofit Doubles as Donor Network for Politicians Giving It Contracts

Executives of the Acacia Network, which does hundreds of millions of dollars worth of business with the city and state, also donate significant funds to local politicians. Photo: Wally Gobetz, flickr, used under a Creative Commons license.

Ben Foldy

The Acacia Network is an organization of non-profit and for-profit subsidiaries that works primarily in services provision for homelessness and substance abuse. Through mergers and absorption it has become the largest Puerto Rican nonprofit in the country, operating primarily in New York City, but also Buffalo, Orlando and Puerto Rico. In New York, the organization earns revenues of over $150 million dollars each year on city contracts and fee-for-service payments from city, state and federal governments.

Beyond spending over $100,000 each year on lobbying, Acacia executives and other related persons have made over $140,000 in contributions directly to area politicians since 2001. These donations often went to politicians in the South Bronx and Harlem where Acacia had significant investments, but also include city politicians like Bill de Blasio and Scott Stringer, who has a significant oversight role on many of Acacia's contracts in his position as city comptroller.

"In New York City there is an intertwining of elected officials and the non-profit sectors that gets described by its advocates as a collaborative effort and is largely in reality simply patronage that you canâ€™t do through government sources anymore,"" said Thad Calabrese, associate professor of public and nonprofit financial management at NYU's Wagner School of Public Service.

To illustrate the point, search the name "Arroyo" in the database below. It will return instances both in which Maria Del Carmen Arroyo is a donor and ones in which she is a recipient of funds. Arroyo began in the nonprofit sector as a director at Narco Freedom. She then ran the South Bronx Community Corporation, before being elected a city councilmember for the South Bronx in 2009 and re-elected in 2013. Arroyo received funds from Acacia executives or former executives in both campaigns. Arroyo resigned her seat on the council mid-term in 2015 and joined Acacia as a vice president. In 2016, she donated to the campaigns of both Bronx borough president Ruben Diaz Jr. and her mother, State Assemblywoman Carmen Arroyo while a salaried executive at Acacia.
